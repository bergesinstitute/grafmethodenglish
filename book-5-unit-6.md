# Used to / be used to / get used to

## Three different structures (tres estructuras diferentes)

These three expressions look similar but have completely different meanings.
This is one of the most confusing topics for English learners.

🔹 Estas tres expresiones se parecen pero tienen significados completamente diferentes.
Este es uno de los temas más confusos para los estudiantes de inglés.

## Used to + infinitive (review)

We learned this in Book 3. It describes past habits that are no longer true:

🔹 Esto lo aprendimos en el Libro 3. Describe hábitos pasados que ya no son ciertos:

*I used to live in Madrid.* (Antes vivía en Madrid). => I don't live there anymore.

*She used to smoke.* (Ella antes fumaba). => She doesn't smoke anymore.

## Be used to + noun / gerund (estar acostumbrado a)

*Be used to* means to be accustomed to something. It describes something that feels
normal to you because you have been doing it for a while:

🔹 *Be used to* significa estar acostumbrado a algo. Describe algo que te resulta
normal porque llevas un tiempo haciéndolo:

*I am used to waking up early.* (Estoy acostumbrado a despertarme temprano).

*She is used to the cold weather.* (Ella está acostumbrada al frío).

*They are used to working long hours.* (Están acostumbrados a trabajar muchas horas).

> After *be used to*, we use a noun or a gerund (verb + *-ing*), never an infinitive.
> *I am used to waking up early.* Not: *I am used to wake up early.*

> 🔹 Después de *be used to*, usamos un sustantivo o un gerundio (verbo + *-ing*), nunca un infinitivo.
> *I am used to waking up early.* No: *I am used to wake up early.*

## Get used to + noun / gerund (acostumbrarse a)

*Get used to* means to become accustomed to something. It describes the process
of adapting to something new:

🔹 *Get used to* significa acostumbrarse a algo. Describe el proceso de adaptación
a algo nuevo:

*I'm getting used to my new job.* (Me estoy acostumbrando a mi nuevo trabajo).

*She can't get used to the traffic.* (Ella no puede acostumbrarse al tráfico).

*He got used to living alone after a few months.*
(Se acostumbró a vivir solo después de unos meses).

## Comparison (comparación)

    I used to drive to work. (Antes iba en coche al trabajo). => past habit, no longer true

    I am used to driving to work. (Estoy acostumbrado a ir en coche al trabajo). => it feels normal now

    I'm getting used to driving to work. (Me estoy acostumbrando a ir en coche al trabajo). => still adapting

# Make vs. do

## The difference (la diferencia)

In Spanish, both *make* and *do* translate as *hacer.* In English, they are different:

🔹 En español, tanto *make* como *do* se traducen como *hacer*. En inglés, son diferentes:

### Do - for activities and tasks (para actividades y tareas)

    do homework - hacer deberes

    do the dishes - fregar los platos

    do the laundry ['lɔndri] - hacer la colada

    do the housework - hacer las tareas del hogar

    do exercise - hacer ejercicio

    do business - hacer negocios

    do a favor ['feɪvər] - hacer un favor

    do your best - hacer lo mejor posible

    do research [rɪ'sərtʃ] - hacer investigación

    do damage ['dæmɪdʒ] - hacer daño

### Make - for creating or producing (para crear o producir)

    make breakfast / lunch / dinner - hacer el desayuno / la comida / la cena

    make a decision - tomar una decisión

    make a mistake - cometer un error

    make money - ganar dinero

    make a phone call - hacer una llamada

    make noise - hacer ruido

    make an effort - hacer un esfuerzo

    make progress ['prɑgrɛs] - hacer progreso

    make a promise - hacer una promesa

    make friends - hacer amigos

    make plans - hacer planes

> There is no perfect rule. These are collocations that you learn with practice.
> In general, *do* is for actions/tasks and *make* is for creating/producing.

> 🔹 No hay una regla perfecta. Son colocaciones que se aprenden con la práctica.
> En general, *do* es para acciones/tareas y *make* es para crear/producir.

# Conversation

María: How's the new job?

Roberto: I'm slowly getting used to it. The hours are very different - I'm not
used to waking up at 5 AM. I used to start work at 9.

M: That must be hard. Are you getting used to the commute [kə'mju:t] (trayecto)?

R: Not yet. I can't get used to driving for an hour every morning.
I used to walk to my old office in ten minutes.

M: Have you made any friends at work?

R: Yes, I've been making an effort to talk to everyone. My colleagues
[kɑli:gz] (compañeros) are very nice. But I still have to do a lot of things
on my own because I'm new.

M: Are you used to the workload ['wɜrkloʊd] (carga de trabajo)?

R: I'm getting used to it. They do things very differently from my old company.
I used to do everything on paper, but here they make us use an app for everything.
I'm not used to working with so much technology.

M: You'll get used to it. I had the same experience when I changed jobs.
After a few months, everything felt normal.

R: I hope so. I should do my best to adapt quickly.

# A note on word stress in multi-syllable words (nota sobre el acento en palabras largas)

In English, every word with more than one syllable has one syllable that is stressed more
than the others. Unlike Spanish, English doesn't use written accent marks, so you have
to learn the stress pattern for each word.

🔹 En inglés, cada palabra con más de una sílaba tiene una sílaba que se acentúa más
que las demás. A diferencia del español, el inglés no usa tildes, así que hay que
aprender el patrón de acento de cada palabra.

Here are some general tendencies:

🔹 Estas son algunas tendencias generales:

### Two-syllable nouns and adjectives: usually stressed on the first syllable

    'table, 'doctor, 'happy, 'water, 'money, 'picture

### Two-syllable verbs: often stressed on the second syllable

    de'cide, a'gree, be'gin, for'get, pre'tend, a'rrive

### Some words change meaning with stress

    'record (noun - disco / registro) vs. re'cord (verb - grabar)

    'present (noun - regalo) vs. pre'sent (verb - presentar)

    'object (noun - objeto) vs. ob'ject (verb - objetar)
